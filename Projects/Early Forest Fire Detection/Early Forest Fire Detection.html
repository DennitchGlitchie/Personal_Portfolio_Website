Early Forest Fire Detection

Summary:

This research was aimed to combat the $200 million in wildfire damages in California and damages all around the world. By placing smart-video sentries in remote and rural locations that are prone to wildfires, the ability to react quickly and prevent fire-spread is greatly enhanced. This study develops a reliable smoke-detection algorithm using Digital Image Processing and Computer Vision techniques on multi-spectral (visible & infrared) video. By utilizing Principal Component Analysis (PCA) followed by spatial filtering and an environment-tuned selective thresholding, the temporal variance of smoke can be accurately identified. Other topics such as spectral signature of smoke and spectral clustering are investigated. This study was used as culminating thesis project and defense to earn my MS in EE at Cal Poly San Luis Obispo. Early Forest Fire Detection via Principal Component Analysis of Spectral and Temporal Smoke Signature is archived in Digital Commons.

300 GB of sample video data captured the burning of various native fuels in a BBQ from 875 yards away. A series of Digital Image Processing techniques were performed to extract, enhance, and match the field of view of the visible and infrared images. Identifying Smoke from Temporal Signature:

A technique based on the Principal Component Transform was used in attempt to extract the unique temporal variance of smoke in a fire-scene. A Principal Component Transform is a statistical procedure that uses an orthogonal transformation to convert correlated images into linearly uncorrelated Principal Component Images. Smoke areas (or areas of strong temporal variance) upon visual inspection can be seen in the Principal Component Images as darker or lighter regions. Using an environment-calibrated thresholding method and post-processing, an algorithm based on Principal Component Analysis was developed to identify smoke in an image of a fire scene.

Investigation of Spectral Smoke Signature: 

Images were transformed into their feature space and analyzed critically to identify whether a "Spectral Smoke Signature" could be uniquely identified. Hue, saturation, and illumination were investigated with various types of cluster analysis including centroid-based, connectivity-based, and density-based clustering. It was determined that smoke in an image could not be easily identified solely by its spectral features. Moreover, infrared data proved to be ineffective here as smoke does not reflect infrared light. 

Visible Spectral Signature

This data plot represents all pixels in a single image represented in the feature space. The black markers represent "smoke pixels" while the red markers make up the rest of the image. Notice that it is difficult to identify smoke purely by its "smoke signature" as the feature space is quite continuous.

In order to optimize smoke identification results, effort was made to identify optimal input parameters  (i. e. number of images, image capture rate, principal component selection).
